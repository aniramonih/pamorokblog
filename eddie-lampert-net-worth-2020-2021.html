Early Life & Biography

In 1962, Lampert was born to Dolores Lampert and Floyd M. Lampert, who was a housewife while his father was a senior partner in the law firm of Lampert & Lampert in New York City.

Also, Lampert has a younger sister Tracey. Lampert’s grandmother instilled in him an interest in investing as she was a passive investor and big fan of Louis Rukeyser’s Wall Street Week television program.

Unfortunately, his father died, which forced his mother to take a job as a clerk at Saks Fifth Avenue. Besides, Lampert worked after school and on weekends at various warehouses, to help support his family. Despite working, he received good grades. Consequently, he received financial support to pay for college.

In 1984, Lampert graduated from Yale University with a B.A., economics, summa cum laude.

Career

In July 1984, Lampert served as an intern at Goldman Sachs and proceeded to work in the firm’s risk arbitrage department from March 1985 to February 1988.

Lampert formed ESL Investments, based in Greenwich In April 1988. His investment style drew comparisons to the financier Warren Buffett.

His earnings were estimated to be $1.02 billion in 2004, which made him the first Wall Street financial manager to exceed an income of $1 billion in a single year.

Lampert featured on the Time 100 list for most influential people in the world for being one of the “brightest minds on Wall Street” and leading a new class of activist hedge funds in 2006. In 2006 Lampert became the wealthiest person in Connecticut with a net worth of $3.8 billion.

In March 2012, Lampert appeared as number 367 on the Forbes world wealthiest people list with a net worth of $3.1 billion. Unfortunately, Lampert had fallen to No. 810 on the list, with a net worth of $2.2 billion by August 2016.

In May 2013, Lampert took over as chief executive officer at Sears, and in July 2016, he had 28% of shares in Sears Holdings Corp worth approximately $408 million.

He received criticism from employees and corporate staff for “slicing” his employees in departmental meetings and “being out of touch with reality.” Sears lost around half its worth within five years and shut down for more than half of its physical stores during his tenure as CEO.

On 15 October 2018, Lampert resigned from being CEO of Sears Holdings but remained as Chairman of the Board. Lampert resigned from being chairman of Sears Holdings Corp on 14 February 2019 According to a company filing.

In January 2019, an assemblage of Sears’ creditors claimed that Lampert had coordinated a “multiyear and multifaceted scheme” to strip away the company’s possessions and benefit from its degeneration to persuade a federal judge to force Sears to liquidate.
